The QARP Method

Quality At a Reasonable Price — how a Shariah-compliant equity desk turns judgment into a single, honest number.

Method Edition A plain-language guide to everything under the score Capital Preservation First

One number, two questions: is the business good, and is the price fair?

Every company we follow runs the same gauntlet. First a hard gate — is it permissible to own at all? Then two independent readings: how strong the business is, and how cheap it trades against what it is worth. QARP fuses those two into a 0–100 score and a plain verdict, from Strongest to Strong Avoid.

The discipline is deliberately conservative. A great company at a punchy price is not a buy; a mediocre one at a giveaway price rarely is either. We would rather miss a winner than lose capital on a value trap — so the method is built to be harder to please than the crowd, and to say exactly why on every name.

The whole method, in one line

QARP = weighted blend of Quality and Price

QARP = w · Quality (/105 → %)  +  1−w · DCF value (/5 → %)

The weight w isn't fixed — it depends on company size (big caps 60/40, sliding to 75/25 for the smallest). Set a size and drag the two sliders; the same business scores differently.

I
The first gate · pass / fail

The Shariah screen

Before a company is ever scored, it must clear an AAOIFI-standard screen — verified through Musaffa and cross-checked against Muslim Xchange, with the stricter view winning any disagreement. A name that fails gets no QARP score and never enters the ranking. This gate is absolute: no quality or price can buy a way past it.

A handful of names are held to a stricter standard than the screeners themselves, by house policy — permanently excluded even where a provider would pass them.

The four tests

  • Permissible core business — no interest/lending, alcohol, tobacco, pork, gambling, adult content, or weapons
  • Impermissible income under 5% of revenue
  • Interest-bearing debt under 30% of market cap
  • Interest-bearing cash & securities under 30% of market cap

III
The price half · 25–40% of the score

Price — the DCF value score, 1 to 5

One number for a single question: how cheap is the stock against an estimate of its fair value? We don't trust any one model. We triangulate three — an intrinsic DCF (AlphaSpread), the analyst consensus target, and a fair-value cross-check (GuruFocus) — and let the most conservative leg win. A first-time score is capped at 4, never 5.

Why three sources, not one

A DCF is only as good as its assumptions. An intrinsic model can miss a re-rating; an analyst can be a cheerleader; a fair-value engine can anchor on a lost multiple. Reading all three and taking the stricter answer is how the score stays honest when they disagree — which is exactly when a single model is most dangerous.

New this edition · the two-P/E lens

A single P/E lies about a cyclical

A trailing P/E is history; a forward P/E is the estimate. When they diverge, the gap is the story — so we now show both, on every name. When earnings are about to surge, the trailing number makes a cheap stock look expensive.

MU 23.4 → 6.9 Micron. Trailing 23× looks dear; forward 7× is dirt cheap — the memory super-cycle in two numbers. Anchor on the trailing figure and you'd wrongly call it expensive.
The rules of the house · where a naïve model goes wrong

Two blind spots we correct for — every time

Standing corrections · coded into the build, audited on every rebuild
CYCLICAL → FORWARD-ANCHOR
Cyclicals are judged on forward earnings

Trailing-earnings models distort any cyclical — at a trough they look expensive when they're cheap; at a peak, cheap when they're dear. So for every cyclical cohort (energy, chemicals, materials, homebuilders, autos, semis…) we anchor the DCF on forward earnings + analyst, not the trailing figure. A build-time audit flags any cyclical that slips through on a trailing anchor, so nothing drifts.

AI-SOFTWARE → CAPPED AT 4
Disruption risk can't be priced as cheapness

Fair-value engines read an AI-disruption discount as a bargain. It isn't — a moat under threat is a risk, not a sale. So software exposed to that risk is capped at DCF 4: it can be a Strong Buy, but never rated deep-value on a triangulation the market may be right to discount.

The score is a snapshot — and the snapshot moves

How a price drop re-rates a stock, live

A DCF score is a judgment made at a known price. We hold the fair value fixed from that moment, then re-band it against the live price every day — so as a stock gets cheaper, its value score rises on its own, no re-scoring required. Here is the exact path Micron walked.

A daily price move re-ranks a name; only a deliberate re-score — on news or fundamentals — resets the fair-value anchor.

IV
Why size changes the mix

The blend slides with company size

Big companies are heavily analyzed, so their value read is reliable and earns the full 40%. Smaller companies are systematically lower-quality, and their cash-flow forecasts are far noisier — so the weight slides toward business quality and balance-sheet strength and away from a fragile price estimate. Down the size scale, surviving and compounding beats chasing a cheap-looking number.

Same business, different score: a cheap-but-fragile micro-cap loses ground versus its large-cap twin, because a low price no longer rescues a weak balance sheet.

Kept beside the score — never mixed into it

The momentum gate: value decides what, the tape decides when

A cheap stock can stay cheap. The gate reads the price trend so a Buy is actionable when the tape agrees — but it never touches the QARP number. Judgment and timing stay separate on purpose.

GO

Price above its 50-day average — the trend confirms; a Buy is actionable.

TURN

Reclaimed the 20-day but under the 50 — a bottoming attempt, still early.

WAIT

Below both averages — the knife is still falling; the verdict stands, but acting fights the tape.

Keeping ourselves honest

We grade the grader

Every verdict is logged at its entry price and tracked. If the framework works, Strong Buys should out-return Avoids over time. We measure that with an information coefficient — the rank correlation between a name's first verdict and its return since. Positive and rising means the ordering carries real signal; flat or negative is a warning we act on. Real factor ICs are small by nature: a steady +0.05 to +0.10 is a good result, read as a trend, not a single day.
